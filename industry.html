Industry Stories

Before the Research,
the Work

Twelve years in enterprise software — chasing down bad data in client warehouses, shipping product roadmaps, and learning what the textbooks leave out.

I came to academic research the long way around. Before I ever set foot in a PhD program, I spent over a decade inside the operations of global companies — first as a consultant and solution manager deploying supply chain software at i2 Technologies, then as Director of Product Management at Servigistics, building the planning tools that aftermarket service organizations depend on.

This page is not a résumé entry. It's an attempt to connect the dots — to explain why the research questions I pursue feel urgent to me in a way that goes beyond academic curiosity. The problems I study now are problems I once lived inside.

i2 Technologies

Now Blue Yonder · Dallas, TX & Tokyo, Japan

Consultant & Solution Manager

How I Got There

I joined i2 Technologies in the late 1990s when the company was riding the first wave of enterprise supply chain software — before SaaS, before cloud, before the term "digital transformation" existed. i2 was building tools that promised to do something genuinely new: let large manufacturers see across their supply chains in real time, synchronize demand with supply, and make better decisions faster. The ambition was enormous. So were the implementation challenges.

"The software worked beautifully in the demo. The real world had other ideas."

What the Work Actually Looked Like

My job was to sell, configure, and deploy supply chain planning systems at Fortune 500 clients across North America and Asia-Pacific. In practice, this meant spending weeks embedded inside client operations — walking factory floors, sitting with demand planners at 6 a.m., tracing the lineage of a single data field through four legacy systems to figure out why the forecast numbers didn't match reality.

Every implementation was a renegotiation between what the software assumed and what the client's operations actually looked like. Lead times that weren't fixed. Demand signals that were contaminated by promotions, returns, and internal orders. Inventory policies that had been set years ago and never revisited. The model in the system was always cleaner than the world it was supposed to represent.

Tokyo and the Asia-Pacific Years

Part of my time at i2 was based in Tokyo, working with Japanese manufacturers and distributors. Working across language and cultural boundaries added another layer to an already complex job. In Japan, the relationship between client and vendor carried different expectations — the implementation was as much a trust-building exercise as a technical one. You didn't just configure a system; you became part of the client's planning team for the duration of the project.

Those years shaped how I think about information asymmetry and incentive alignment in supply chains — topics that would eventually show up in my research. When a client's planning team doesn't fully share operational data with their software vendor, when they paper over problems to make the project look on track, the downstream consequences can take months to surface. I watched it happen more than once.

What I Took Away

Supply chain theory says: minimize costs subject to service-level constraints, given some demand distribution and lead time. Practice says: figure out which data you can actually trust, negotiate with stakeholders who have competing incentives, and build a model that's wrong in predictable rather than unpredictable ways. The gap between those two statements is where most of the real difficulty lives — and it's a gap that academic research can help close, if it takes the operational messiness seriously.

Servigistics

Acquired by PTC · Atlanta, GA

Director of Product Management

Moving from Consulting to Product

After years of deploying other people's software, I wanted to sit on the other side of the table — to be responsible for what the product actually did, not just how it was implemented. Servigistics gave me that chance. The company built planning software specifically for aftermarket service organizations: the parts, logistics, and planning operations that keep equipment running after the original sale.

It's a domain that doesn't get as much attention as forward supply chains, but the operational complexity is just as high — and in many ways, the stakes are higher. When a hospital's imaging equipment goes down, or an airline's ground service vehicle needs a part, the tolerance for stockouts is effectively zero. The cost of carrying too much inventory is also enormous. Service parts planning sits in that narrow window between the two.

"In service parts, the demand signal is rare, erratic, and safety-critical. You can't average your way to a good forecast."

Service Parts Demand Forecasting

Most demand forecasting assumes items sell regularly enough to detect a pattern. Service parts violate that assumption constantly. A critical spare part might have zero demand for six months, then a sudden spike when a fleet ages or a recall hits. The statistical methods that work well for consumer goods — moving averages, exponential smoothing, seasonal decomposition — are often useless here.

As product lead for the forecasting module, I worked directly with data scientists and domain experts to define the right methods for intermittent demand — Croston's method, Syntetos-Boylan approximations, and eventually machine learning approaches that could incorporate equipment age, operating environment, and maintenance history as signals. Getting the forecasting wrong didn't just create excess inventory; it created field failures that damaged the client's reputation with their own customers.

Multi-Echelon Inventory Optimization

A large service organization might have parts stocked at a central depot, several regional warehouses, and hundreds of field technician vehicles — a three- or four-echelon network. The classic single-location inventory model doesn't come close to capturing this. The optimization problem involves not just how much to stock at each location, but how to balance stock across levels to minimize both cost and response time simultaneously.

Working on this product gave me a deep appreciation for how far the gap between the academic literature and practical implementation can run. The theoretically optimal solution often required data that didn't exist, or computational time that wasn't acceptable, or assumptions about demand distributions that simply didn't hold. Building a product meant making principled compromises — and being honest with clients about where those compromises were.

Reverse Logistics

Service parts don't just flow out — they flow back. A failed part gets replaced and returned. Cores get refurbished and re-enter the supply chain. Warranties generate reverse flows that need to be tracked, sorted, and valued. The reverse logistics module was in many ways the most underdeveloped part of the product when I took it on, partly because the problem is genuinely hard — the condition of returned parts is uncertain, the timing is unpredictable, and the economic value of a returned core depends on the cost of sourcing a new one.

The operational complexity of reverse logistics in a service context was part of what eventually drew me toward research on remanufacturing, warranty strategy, and the circular economy. These aren't just supply chain optimization problems — they involve information asymmetry, moral hazard, and questions about how firms design incentives for customers and service providers. That's where the economics gets interesting.

The Research Question I Couldn't Answer on the Job

By the time I left Servigistics to pursue a PhD, I had a list of questions I couldn't answer from inside a product organization. Why do firms systematically underinvest in service parts planning even when the ROI is easy to demonstrate? How does the structure of warranty contracts shape the incentives of service providers — and through them, the operational decisions that determine whether customers get good service? What does "transparency" mean in a supply chain where every participant has information the others don't?

Those questions led me to Georgia Tech, to the work of Ravi Subramanian and Atalay Atasu, and eventually to the research agenda I'm still pursuing. The industry years didn't just inform my research — they gave it its urgency.

Why It Matters for Research & Teaching

Most operations management research begins with a model — a stylized representation of a supply chain, a contract, a firm's decision problem. The art of building a good model is knowing which simplifications to make and which to resist. My industry experience gives me a calibration for that judgment that I couldn't have developed from the literature alone.

In the classroom, it means I can tell students not just what the theory says, but where the theory breaks — and why that matters. The student who goes on to manage a service parts network will not face a Poisson demand process; they'll face a messy mixture of intermittent demand, legacy data, and organizational politics. Knowing that the model is an approximation — and a useful one — is a different kind of knowledge than knowing the model.

The decade-plus I spent in industry is not a detour from the academic path. It is the reason the academic questions I ask are the ones I ask.
